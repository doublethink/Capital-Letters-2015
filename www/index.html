Glenn Colquhoun

Glenn Colquhoun is a poet and children's writer. His first collection The art of walking upright won the Jessie Mackay best first book of poetry award at the 2000 Montana book awards. Playing God, his third collection, won the poetry section of the same awards in 2003 as well as the reader's choice award that year. He has written four children's books and published a book of essays entitled Jumping ship and other essays.

He was awarded the Prize in modern letters in 2004 and a Fulbright scholarship to Harvard University in 2010. In 2012 he was part of the ‘Transit of Venus’ poetry exchange at the Frankfurt book fair and in 2014 represented New Zealand on the Commonwealth Poets United poetry project which celebrated the Glasgow Commonwealth Games that year.

He works as a GP in Horowhenua.

Karen is a senior consultant with CORE Education (Twitter: @virtuallykaren) and has been in education for 20+ years. Karlo Mila

Karlo Mila is a poet, writer and researcher of Tongan and Palangi descent who grew up in Palmerston North. An award-winning poet, she has published two books of poetry and is widely anthologized. “Dream Fish Floating” won the best first book of poetry prize at the Montana Book Awards 2005. Karlo's PhD focused on the culture, identity and wellbeing of NZ-born Pacific population.

Her postdoctoral research involved developing “Mana Moana” an indigenous wellbeing intervention targeted at Pacific young people. This collated widely shared Pacific archetypes, generative concepts, words, associated proverbs, narratives, and myths, and underpinned by Pacific philosophies of health and wellbeing. This has been woven into an accessible group-work programme called “the Journey to Motutapu" (sacred islands). The aim was to ignite and transmit ancestral knowledge to Pacific youth and strengthen cultural identity and sense of legacy and purpose. This programme was tested for feasibility and accessibility with Wesley College prefects.

Karlo has an MSW (Applied) and worked as the Manager, Pacific Health Research, at the Health Research Council of New Zealand. She has been an Op-Ed columnist for the Dominion-Post, worked as a Union Organiser and was secondary school teacher in Tonga. Karlo currently works at Le Va, an NGO specialising in Pacific-centred projects in the field of mental health, addictions, disability and public health.

Karlo lives in Newtown, Wellington with her two boys.

Memorise word classes via boxing moves, if you're game - Karen Wilson

Location: BG5

Have you considered ditching the textbook or homework book when teaching parts of speech? How can students remember all eight terms and what their purpose is in a sentence? You've heard of the poems, songs and raps that are supposed to help students, but if students aren't active in their own learning process, how will their recall fare the following year, or the year after that?

In this session, you will actively participate in a memory technique whereby each part of speech will be paired with a boxing move. Together they become a memory peg (a concept developed by Dr Rich Allen. ULearn13).

By the end of the session, you will have a template sentence using all parts of speech. Once taught, this technique needs to be repeated as a drill or a “warm-up” with students working in pairs. Alternatively, students can face the same direction and punch whichever sequence the teacher calls, all at the same time.

It has potential to be developed further to teach sentence types.

Adolescent Boys - Getting the Buggers to Read - John and Ruth McIntyre

85% of the primary school teachers, 60% of the secondary school teachers, and probably 98% of the school librarians in New Zealand are women, so where do our boys find their reading role models?

We often hear about boys lagging behind girls in literacy statistics at secondary school, but is it more a case of them not being presented with books on subjects they want to read about?

Children’s bookseller, reviewer and awards judge John McIntyre has been both a boy and a teacher, and is highly opinionated on the vexed subject of boys and reading.

In this session he will discuss the issues around the topic and, with his wife Ruth, will present a range of titles and strategies that can and do, turn boys onto books.

They will leave you with a list of their favourite titles and invite attendees to add theirs, and give you a new and positive perspective of the reading habits of the adolescent male of our species.

Reciprocal teaching as a tool to enhance understanding of unfamiliar texts - Jenny MacDonald

Location: B13

Reciprocal Teaching is a reading comprehension strategy proven to have a significant impact on a student’s ability to better engage with and gain meaning from unfamiliar texts. Students construct shared meaning through group discussion as they predict, question, clarify and summarise. As a student gains confidence in the use of strategies that effective readers use and internalises these, they are more able to apply them to their own independent reading.

Students are regularly required to read challenging and unfamiliar texts across all learning areas, however, there are standards in English that specifically assess a student’s ability to comprehend the purpose and audience, ideas, language features and/or structure of unfamiliar texts. This workshop will explore the use of reciprocal teaching as a tool to enhance student understanding in those standards and it will also consider it as a strategy to develop the skills and confidence that students require to make meaning of unfamiliar written material in English and across the curriculum.

“This is me and this is all the butterflies”: Student identity in differing school contexts - Liana MacDonald and Dr. Gillian Hubbard

Location: B14

The Senior English Curriculum Guide places a firm emphasis on “identity” as a core aspect of English teaching. In two separate pieces of research on teaching in the secondary English classroom in New Zealand, the two presenters of this workshop, PhD candidate and English teacher Liana MacDonald and her supervisor at Victoria University Gillian Hubbard, heard differing interpretations and responses to Patricia Grace’s iconic short story Butterflies, from differing school contexts. We have been thinking as a result about those issues of student ability to express, or even reveal, their own perspectives and beliefs in the diverse contexts of teaching in New Zealand. In this workshop we will play around with some poems, short stories and extracts from novels taught in the English classroom, thinking in an exploratory way about identity and sometimes hidden identity.

Just the boys for this game: letters from the trenches - Steve Watters

Location: B23

On 1 July 1916, Wellington College old boy Alex McColl writing to his mother from France, described ‘today as a red letter day in my history…. I am taking out a raiding party against the Huns.’ He considered himself ‘the luckiest of men to have the opportunity of leading such a splendid body of men.’ He continued, ‘I have most of the old football team here including some very well-known reps, just the boys for this game.’ Within 24 hours of writing this letter he was dead. Hundreds if not thousands of similar letters exist. Can they be viewed as attempts to provide succour to families at home in times of despair or an important glimpse into the thoughts, emotions and motivations of those who went to war?

Philosophy in English - Gareth Cass

Location: BG5

What does Philosophy have to do with English?

I would argue that we already teach Philosophy in English. Also that doing so explicitly and consciously can help our students to increase their engagement in our subject and the depth of their thinking.

The seminar will cover an overview of the value of teaching philosophical concepts in English and demonstrate methods of introducing these ideas with practical and interactive exercises.

Material covered includes - Teaching the difference between rhetoric and logical argument. Logical fallacies. How to use textual analysis as a springboard for exploring ideas in depth. Engaging students in the reality of ideas through discussion and problem solving activities. Student guidance of the inquiry process, leading to greater engagement in and ownership of the ideas and themes of texts.

The seminar will be split between a partially interactive presentation of the general topic and practical implementation of some of the techniques. We will work in pairs, small groups and a full class setting.

A range of resources will be discussed and provided to suit classes across a range of year levels.

To Teach or Not to Teach: The Ugliness of Life - Lynda Scott Araya

Location: B13

Although we might agree about the sensitivities involved, there is much debate about whether we should teach texts that deal with the topics or allow our learners to research them. We consider whether to create/preserve an illusion of childhood; whether to provide a space of gritty reality or whether, for personal reasons to avoid such topics.

In both my personal life and within my teaching contexts, I have had to confront such decisions and to work through the inherent challenges.

In this workshop, I will outline some of my ideas and experiences and to open up the floor for a discussion on the pros and cons of addressing whanau/intimate partner violence and dysfunction as well as helpful teaching strategies.
