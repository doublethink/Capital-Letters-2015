Speakers

Glenn Colquhoun

Glenn Colquhoun is a poet and children's writer. His first collection The art of walking upright won the Jessie Mackay best first book of poetry award at the 2000 Montana book awards. Playing God, his third collection, won the poetry section of the same awards in 2003 as well as the reader's choice award that year. He has written four children's books and published a book of essays entitledJumping ship and other essays. He was awarded the Prize in modern letters in 2004 and a Fulbright scholarship to Harvard University in 2010. In 2012 he was part of the ‘Transit of Venus’ poetry exchange at the Frankfurt book fair and in 2014 represented New Zealand on the Commonwealth Poets United poetry project which celebrated the Glasgow Commonwealth Games that year. He works as a GP in Horowhenua.

Bernard Beckett

Bernard Beckett is a school teacher and writer who has been working in schools in the Wellington region for the past twenty five years. His books have been published in over thirty countries, and translated into more than twenty languages. His latest novel, Lullaby, will be published in May.

Karen Melhuish Spencer

Karen is a senior consultant with CORE Education (Twitter: @virtuallykaren) and has been in education for 20+ years. With a background in English and Drama, she is now internationally recognised for her work in e-learning, blended/online professional facilitation and online community development. She is an inspiring presenter, known for her energy, humour and research-led approach.

Karen currently leads the Connected Learning Advisory-Te Ara Whītiki, supporting all New Zealand schools and kura to use digital technologies. She spearheaded New Zealand’s inaugural Connected Educator Month in 2014 and has undertaken extensive work in curriculum and assessment projects for the Ministry of Education, NZQA, and for international agencies. She was central to the development of Enabling e-Learning, the VLN Groups social network and the e-Learning Planning Framework. In 2013, Karen received the inaugural ICET Asia-Pacific Senior e-Fellow award.

Waving or drowning? Thriving in a world of listicles, astroturfers and instachat

In a word of near ubiquitous access to the information worldwide, the way we think about learning is changing exponentially. (R)evolutions in our understanding of how we grasp new ideas, combined with the way we use digital technologies, means that educators are reimagining what communication, creation and school environments might be. If our students can access what they need when they need it online, publishing multimedia at the touch of a button, what does this mean for the way our students learn about making and creating meaning? We can talk to colleagues across the world through social networks 24-7 - so what does it mean for professional learning to pull up a chair in a virtual staffroom?

Karen will paint a picture of changing practices and new possibilities for the education profession and invite you to consider the place of the English teacher within it.

Karlo Mila

Karlo Mila is a poet, writer and researcher of Tongan and Palangi descent who grew up in Palmerston North. An award-winning poet, she has published two books of poetry and is widely anthologized. “Dream Fish Floating” won the best first book of poetry prize at the Montana Book Awards 2005. Karlo's PhD focused on the culture, identity and wellbeing of NZ-born Pacific population.

Her postdoctoral research involved developing “Mana Moana” an indigenous wellbeing intervention targeted at Pacific young people. This collated widely shared Pacific archetypes, generative concepts, words, associated proverbs, narratives, and myths, and underpinned by Pacific philosophies of health and wellbeing. This has been woven into an accessible group-work programme called “the Journey to Motutapu" (sacred islands). The aim was to ignite and transmit ancestral knowledge to Pacific youth and strengthen cultural identity and sense of legacy and purpose. This programme was tested for feasibility and accessibility with Wesley College prefects.

Karlo has an MSW (Applied) and worked as the Manager, Pacific Health Research, at the Health Research Council of New Zealand. She has been an Op-Ed columnist for the Dominion-Post, worked as a Union Organiser and was secondary school teacher in Tonga. Karlo currently works at Le Va, an NGO specialising in Pacific-centred projects in the field of mental health, addictions, disability and public health. Karlo lives in Newtown, Wellington with her two boys.
